一篇论文的分析探讨

Posted on 2020-05-30 In 论文 Views: Valine:

“Evaluating the employment impact of a mandatory job search program” by Richard Blundell, Monica Costa-Dias, Costas Meghir and John van Reenen.

Please, read the paper and answer the following questions:

1. What treatment do the authors evaluate? What is the outcome variable?

Answer 1.1: The authors evaluate the treatment effects of a labor market program called“New Deal for the Young Unemployed”in the UK ,including substitution effects and equilibrium wage effects. The authors especially focus on the degree to which the “Gateway” has lead to an increase in outflows into employment.

Answer 1.2: The outcome variable is the proportion leaving unemployment within eight or ten months of entering the unemployment pool. Specifically, it is ,a discrete variable indicating whether an individual left unemployment or not between the sixth and eighth month of an unemployment spell.


2. Table 1 shows some of the main estimation results. Write down the regression equation shown in column 1 and explain!

Answer 2: The regression equation is: \[Y_{i t}=\theta_{N D}+d_{t}+\gamma X_{i t}+\alpha N D_{i t}+\varepsilon_{i t}\] -   \(Y_{i t}\) is a discrete variable indicating whether the individual left unemployment between the sixth and eighth month of an unemployment spell.

-  \(N D_{i t}\) is the treatment×time variable. \(N D_{i t}=1\) if the person is in the treatment group and the time period is after the reform.

-   \(X_{i t}\) are some controls to correct for differences in observable characteristics, including marital status, sought occupation, region and labor market history (the total number of JSA spells and the proportion of time on JSA over the two years preceding the start of the present spell). Age and the number of JSA spells since 1982 are also included when similar age groups are being compared.

-   \(\theta_{N D}\) is an eligibility specific intercept (it may be defined over area or age or both, depending on the comparison group used ).

-    \(d_{t}\) reflects common/aggregate effects.

-    \(\varepsilon_{i t}\) is the error term.


3. In column 3 of Table 1 the authors show results of a propensity score matching method. Explain the method! What is the advantage of this method compared to OLS estimation in column 1?

Answer 3.1: The column 3 of table1 use the differences-in-differences methodology combined with propensity score matching. The procedures of this method is :

① Compute the two propensity scores: \[P_{E X}=P(N D=1 | X)\] \[P_{t X}=P(t=1 | X)\] ② Match the individuals in treatment group with individuals in control group according to the propensity scores.

③ Compute the difference \(Y_{1 i}^{t_{1}}-Y_{1 i}^{t_{0}}\) for every individual in treatment group.

④ Compute the difference \(Y_{0 j}^{t_{1}}-Y_{0 j}^{t_{0}}\) for every matched individual in control group in \(I_{0} \cap S_{p}\).

⑤ Then we can get the treatment effects: \[\tau_{A T T}^{P S M-D I D}=\frac{1}{n_{1}} \sum_{i \in I_{1} \cap S_{p}}\left\{\left(Y_{1 i}^{t_{1}}-Y_{1 i}^{t_{0}}\right)-\sum_{i \in I_{0} \cap S_{p}} W(i, j)\left(Y_{0 j}^{t_{1}}-Y_{0 j}^{t_{0}}\right)\right\}\] -   \(S_{p}\) is the set of common support; \(I_{1}=\left\{i: N D_{i}=1\right\}\) (the set of treatment group); \(I_{0}=\left\{j: N D_{j}=0\right\}\) (the set of comparison group);

-   \(n_{1}\) is the number of samples in \(I_{1} \cap S_{p}\); \(t_{1}\) is the time period after the policy; \(t_{0}\) is the time period before the policy; \(W(i, j)\) is the weight.

-    \(Y_{1 i}^{t_{1}}-Y_{1 i}^{t_{0}}\) is the difference of the outcome variable before and after the policy for individual in treatment group; \(Y_{0 j}^{t_{1}}-Y_{0 j}^{t_{0}}\) is the difference of the outcome variable before and after the policy for individual in comparison group.

Answer 3.2: The choices of Pathfinder areas and time are non-random. Relative to the OLS method, the propensity score matching can obtain an average impact across different effects, and balance the distribution of the relevant observable characteristics X in the treatment and control groups. It improves the similarity between the treated and the controls. And the differences-in-differences methodology combined with propensity score matching can control unobservable and time invariant differences between groups.


4. What are the alternative treatment and control groups? What is the advantage of looking at multiple control groups?

Answer 4.1: There are 8 comparisons in table 1.

  •   In row1-row4, the treatment group is 19-24 year olds living in Pathfinder areas, and the control groups are 19-24 year olds living in all non-Pathfinder areas, 19-24 year olds living in matched non-Pathfinder areas, 25-30 year olds living in Pathfinder areas, 31-40 year olds living in Pathfinder areas respectively.

  •   In row5-row6, the treatment group is 25-30 year olds living in Pathfinder areas, and the control groups are 25-30 year olds living in all other areas and 25-30 year olds living in matched non-Pathfinder areas respectively.

  •   In row7, the treatment group is 19-30 year olds living in Pathfinder areas, and the control group is 19-30 year olds living in all other areas.

  •   In row 8, the treatment group is 19-50 year olds living Pathfinder areas, and the control group is 19-50 year olds living in all other areas.

Answer 4.2: There are several advantages looking at multiple control groups.

①Assess the indirect effects including the substitution and equilibrium wage effects.

       --     Take the column 1 as an example, the comparison in row3 may suffer from substitution more acutely than rows 1 and 2 and it is not immune to equilibrium wage effects. But the estimates are close and insignificantly different. Hence, the null hypothesis of a model without substitution and equilibrium wage effects cannot be rejected. Alternatively, their effects may cancel out.

       --     Then the comparison in row 4 verifies whether the substitution effects exist or not. The control group of 31-40-year-olds living in Pathfinder areas in row 4 should be less substitutable for 19-24-year-olds than younger comparison group in row 3, thus the estimate of row 4 is expected to be lower than that of row3.But the results are not consistent with the expectation. So there doesn’t exist large substitution effects.

       --     The null hypothesis of a model without substitution and equilibrium wage effects cannot be rejected.


②Assess the robustness of the results.(row1-row4 and row7-wor8)

③Satisfy the common trend assumption better. The choices of the comparison groups in rows 2 and 3 make it more likely that their overall characteristics and behavior match that of the treatment group than row 1.

④Rows 5 and 6 play the role of placebo test. No significant effects are found in the two comparisons.


5. Write down the common trend assumption in the notation of the paper and explain!


Answer 5.1: In the parametric methods, the common trend assumption is:

\[\begin{array}{l} f^{-1}\left[E\left(Y_{i t}^{0} | X, N D=1, t=1\right)\right]-f^{-1}\left[E\left(Y_{i t}^{0} | X, N D=1, t=0\right)\right] \\ =f^{-1}\left[E\left(Y_{i t}^{0} | X, N D=0, t=1\right)\right]-f^{-1}\left[E\left(Y_{i t}^{0} | X, N D=0, t=0\right)\right] \end{array}\] In the non-parametric methods, the common trend assumption is:

\[\begin{array}{l} E\left(Y_{i t}^{0} | P_{E X}, P_{t X}, N D=1, t=1\right)-E\left(Y_{i t}^{0} | P_{E X}, P_{t X}, N D=1, t=0\right) \\ =E\left(Y_{i t}^{0} | P_{E X}, P_{t X}, N D=0, t=1\right)-E\left(Y_{i t}^{0} | P_{E X}, P_{t X}, N D=0, t=0\right) \end{array}\] -     \(f^{-1}\) is the inverse probability function;

-     \(Y_{i t}^{0}\) is the outcome for individual in period if not exposed to the policy (treatment);

-     \(P_{E X}=P(N D=1 | X)\) and \(P_{t X}=P(t=1 | X)\);

-     \(X\) is a series of observable characteristics;

-     \(N D=1\) and \(N D=0\) designate the treatment group and the comparison group respectively;

-     \(t=0\) and \(t=1\) represent the period before implementation and the period after respectively.

Answer 5.2: The common trend assumption is that the difference between the treated and comparison group curves should keep constant over time in the absence of the policy. Namely, the expectation of the difference of the outcome variable before and after the policy for the treatment group should be equal to that of the control group in the absence of the policy.

In the parametric methods, it should condition on the observable characteristics X, while in the non-parametric methods, it should condition on the propensity scores for eligibility. On account of the discrete nature of the outcome variable, the assumption do not hold for the expectations but for some transformation, in particular for the inverse of the probability function. The common trend assumption in parametric take the form of inverse probability function in the text.




6. What possible violations of the common trend assumption do the authors consider in this study? How do they test for these violations?


Answer 6.1: There are several possible violations of the common trend assumption.

①There may exist anticipation of the program among eligible individuals. Individuals eligible for the program could react to it in anticipation of the program. If the program is perceived as being able to improve placements, then eligible individuals who are close to the Gateway may reduce their search effort and wait for the program.

②The program is area-specific. Pathfinder areas and non-pathfinder areas may have different labor market conditions, thus may have different trends.

③The program is based on age-related eligibility. Younger group and older group may have different trends.

Answer 6.2: The authors firstly display the pre-trend curves to prejudge whether the treatment groups and control groups have roughly the same trend before the implementation, which is not enough. Then they computed the series of year-by-year difference in difference dynamic estimates of the impact of a fictitious program and plots the estimates of nonexistent programs. And all other estimates for previous periods are insignificant at the 5% level. That is, before the introduction of the New Deal the estimated impacts are zero. So the common trend assumption is satisfied.
